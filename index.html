Biography

I am currently a Lecturer in Computer Science at the University of Bristol, where my teaching and research is about mathematical theories for reasoning about computation and their application in programming tools.

Previously I was a postdoctoral researcher at the Universities of Oxford and Warwick, and I completed my DPhil at the University of Oxford. Prior to academia, I worked for the formal methods group of QinetiQ using mechanised proof assistants as part of verifying the correctness of certain safety critical systems.

Research

My research is on program analysis and automated reasoning, especially for applications in program verification.

  • Tools for functional programming. I work on designing tools that functional programmers can use to reason about their programs. It is very important to my work that tools should be artifacts with predictable behaviour and understandable character, rather than just a button that is pushed with some hope for the best. A good example of this is our forthcoming POPL'21 paper, in which Eddie Jones and I have developed a new refinement type system and inference algorithm that is capable of automatically proving pattern-match safety and comes with a linear-time guarantee on run-time complexity. On the one hand, it is easy to understand when this tool will (or will not) be useful, because it's reasoning power is characterised by a familiar type system; on the other hand there is no concern over termination since scalability is ensured by our complexity argument. You can find our prototype implementation for Haskell, Intensional Datatypes, on GitHub and Hackage.
  • Fragments of higher-order logic. I argue that the natural setting in which to reason about higher-order computation, such as the computation exhibited by functional programs, is higher-order logic. I am interested in fragments of higher-order logic that have good computational properties. In our POPL'18 paper, Toby Cathcart Burn, Luke Ong and I introduced higher-order constrained Horn clauses which are a natural extension of constrained Horn clauses to higher-order logic. The first-order restriction of this logic is already known to be an excellent language in which to discuss program invariants. We showed that the satisfiability problem for our logic, although undecidable, is both a natural language for formalising higher-order program safety and has a number of excellent algorithmic properties.
  • Automata over infinite alphabets. I am interested in understanding the character of finite automata over infinite alphabets. From the perspective of program verification, such automata are useful as a representation of the state spaces of programs manipulating unbounded, named resources. Andrzej Murawski, Nikos Tzevelekos and I characterised the computational complexity of certain important decision problems over a variety of common definitions of register automata. For example, in our LICS'15 paper, we show that the register discipline used determines whether the bisimilarity problem is EXPTIME-, PSPACE- or NP-complete and, in our JCSS article, we showed that, by contrast, the emptiness problem is EXPTIME-complete regardless of discipline. On a more positive note, building on some work in computational group theory, we showed that the language equivalence problem for deterministic register automata (of a certain kind) can be decided in PTIME and this formed the foundation of our tool DEQ, available on GitHub.
  • Higher-order model checking. My doctoral thesis was on the theory and practice of the higher-order model checking problem. Narrowly defined, this is the problem of verifying that the infinite tree defined by a given higher-order recursion scheme satisfies a property definable in the modal mu-calculus or the monadic second-order theory of trees. In our POPL'14 paper, Luke Ong and I gave a very efficient algorithm for deciding the safety restriction of this model checking problem that was based on the idea of using negative types to characterise information coming from spurious counterexamples. In our POPL'11 paper we had already given the first general purpose method for software verification based on this decision problem.

Teaching

I run the following two modules at the University of Bristol:

  • COMS30040: Types and Lambda Calculus. An introduction to the pure, untyped lambda calculus, ML-style type systems and the Curry-Howard correspondence.
  • COMS20007: Programming Languages and Computation (Part I). An introduction to the theory of computation, with a logical flavour. Finite automata, the decidability of Presburger arithmetic, Turing Machines, the Church-Turing thesis, undecidability, reductions and the unsolvability of first-order arithmetic.
